What is kinematics ?


Definition:

Kinematics is a branch of physics that describes the motion of points, objects and systems (group of objects), but without considering the causes of its motion! Basically, it means that we won't talk much about forces or anything such, but mainly about speed, velocities, energies and most importantly: motion.



But why should we study motion ?

Well, objects all around us are in motion! Everything from a simple tennis ball to the Hercules-Corona Borealis Great Wall (the largest known object in the Universe) involves motion one way or another. Interesting questions can thus arise: how long will it take for a space probe to travel to the Moon? What is the best way to shoot a football ?


Kinematics is the key. But in order to better understand kinematics, we will first need to get in touch with some basic concepts, the first one being: frames of reference!




Frames of reference

It is impossible to describe an object's position or speed. Yes, you heard me right. It's impossible. In fact, right now, if you're seated in front of your computer, you might think that your speed is 0. But isn't the Earth actually spinning on itself ? So technically, you're spinning too... But isn't the Earth also rotating around the Sun ? So what's your actual speed ?


Well, maybe we should choose a point in space, and it would be smarter to give your position and speed relative to this point.

For example, if this point is your computer, then maybe that your speed is really 0, but if this point is the Sun, then your speed will be infinitely greater!

If you're in a car going at 20km/h, then your speed relative to the road is 20km/h, but your speed relative to the car you're in will always be 0km/h, since you and the car are going at the same speed.



Well, this is the definition of a frame of reference:

It is an "arbitrary set of axes with reference to which the position or motion of something is described or physical laws are formulated."

Remember this: it is arbitrary. As long as you are consistent, any frame is technically equally valid (with some being more convenient than others). But you don’t want to change coordinate systems in the middle of a calculation.


How to translate this into calculations ?

It's as simple as that: we set the (0,0) of our coordinate system being the point we chose, and the set of axis becomes our frame of reference!

Degrees and Radians

There are two units for measuring an angle: degrees and radians.

Both of them represent exactly the same thing, the only difference being that:

  • In degrees, a circle is 360° (thus half-circle is 180°, right angle is 90°, etc..)
  • In radians, a circle is 2π rad (thus half-circle is π rad, right angle is π⁄2 rad, etc..)

Conversion

Conversion between the two units is thus as easy as that:

  • 1° = π⁄180 rad
  • 1 rad = 180/π°

Their reverses:

With that, we know that we can find the ratio between two sides of the rectangle triangle if we know an angle, but is the reverse true?


Can we find the angle if we know the two sides?


Yes, and for that, we will use their reverse function!

In fact,

  • sin(x) = a⁄c , but sin-1(a⁄c) = x
  • cos(x) = b⁄c , but cos-1(b⁄c) = x
  • tan(x) = a⁄b , but tan-1(a⁄b) = x


Okay, but what does that mean?

Well, it teaches us two things:

  • It is easy to find the sides and angles of a rectangle triangle (which will be very helpful later on)
  • Together, the three trig functions sine, cosine and tangent help us to find "missing informations" about rectangle triangles

Scalars & Vectors

The last building blocks of kinematics are vectors. But what exactly is a vector?


Well, a vector is a very particular type of quantity.

In fact, scalar quantities usually only possess one attribute: magnitude (ex: 3 meters, 4 kilograms, etc...)

But vectors also possess another attribute: direction (and now maybe you start to understand why we talked about angles earlier...)
