What is 2D ?

Until here, we only studied one dimensional motions (URM and UARM) and circular motions (UCM and UACM)

However, a lot of movements are neither one dimensional nor circular. This is the case of thrown balls, canons, and basically most projectiles!

As you can see, projectiles move left and right (x-axis), but also up and down (y-axis)! So, how should we combine the two ?


Gravity on Earth:

An object thrown in the air like a projectile will draw a curve with the shape of a parabola.


This is mainly because of earth's gravity. You can think of it like this: gravity is the object's acceleration towards the ground.

(For more formal definitons, check here)

Another important thing: there is nothing apart from gravity that acts on a projectile.


So, how should we tackle 2D motion ?

Well, this is the key: when you throw an object in the air, it only accelerates towards the ground. This is because gravity only acts vertically on the object, and won't slow down the object horizontally.

It might sound surprising, but any object that you throw never loses "horizontal" speed. Put in another way, at no point will the object move "purely vertically"



To describe 2D motion, we will therefore separate the x and y axis. Because of what we said earlier, the y axis will be a UARM, with acceleration being gravity, while the x axis will be a simple URM, since there is no acceleration towards left or right.


Finding the inital velocities:

The key step in 2D motion will be to find the inital horizontal and vertical velocities. For this, we will have to use trigonometry. In fact, let's say that a projectile is thrown at 14,1 m/s, with an angle of 45°.



On the picture, you can clearly see that the x-component of the speed vector is 10 m/s, and the y-component is also 10 m/s. These are our initial velocities. But so, how do we go from 14.1 m/s with an angle of 45° to 10 m/s and 10 m/s ?


Well, by using sine and cosine ! On the picture, it is clear that sin(45)x14.1 = 10, which is the initial vertical velocity. Similarly, cos(45)x14.1 = 10, which is the initial horizontal velocity (sin gives vertical and cos gives horizontal here).



So, our initial velocities are found using some (quite trivial) trigonometry! What's next ?
